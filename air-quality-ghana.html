Project Overview

Mapping Ghana's Air Pollution Crisis

This final year university research project provides a national-scale assessment of three critical atmospheric pollutants — Carbon Monoxide (CO), Nitrogen Dioxide (NO₂), and Sulphur Dioxide (SO₂) — across all sixteen regions of Ghana. Using satellite remote sensing and Geographic Information Systems, the study maps the spatial distribution of these gases, analyses their seasonal variation across a five-year period, and evaluates their health implications for the Ghanaian population.

The research goes beyond mapping to diagnose the why behind the patterns: why pollutants concentrate in the south, why the dry season is more dangerous, and why certain groups bear a disproportionate health burden. Qualitative interviews with healthcare professionals at Nyaho Medical Centre provide ground-level evidence to complement the satellite-derived quantitative data, producing a genuinely mixed-methods analysis. Supervised by Dr. Ebenezer F. Amankwaa, Department of Geography and Resource Development, University of Ghana.

Methodology

A Multi-Method Approach to Air Quality Assessment

The research combined satellite-derived atmospheric data, GIS spatial analysis, temporal trend mapping, and direct interviews with healthcare practitioners to build a comprehensive picture of air pollution in Ghana.

01

Satellite Data Acquisition

Atmospheric column density data for CO, NO₂, and SO₂ were sourced and processed in Google Earth Engine — each gas dataset was called from the satellite archive, clipped to Ghana's national boundary, and prepared for spatial analysis and time-series extraction.

02

Spatial Analysis & Mapping

Pollutant concentration data processed in GEE were exported and mapped across Ghana's sixteen regions in QGIS, producing spatial distribution maps that reveal regional hotspots and concentration gradients for each gas.

03

Temporal & Seasonal Analysis

Quantitative time-series data for each gas were extracted directly in Google Earth Engine spanning January 2019 to June 2024. The extracted data were then imported into Microsoft Excel to generate the time-series charts and identify seasonal cycles and year-on-year trends.

04

Healthcare Professional Interviews

Semi-structured interviews were conducted with healthcare practitioners at Nyaho Medical Centre, gathering qualitative evidence on observed health conditions, vulnerable populations, and barriers to effective air quality management.

05

Health Implications Assessment

WHO mortality data for Ghana (2010–2019) were analysed alongside the spatial and qualitative findings to quantify the health burden linked to pollutant exposure — covering respiratory infections, cardiovascular disease, lung cancers, and COPD.

Results — Spatial Distribution

Where Pollution Concentrates — and Why

The spatial distribution maps for each gas reveal distinct patterns shaped by urbanisation, industrialisation, and domestic energy use. Comparing 2019 to 2024 shows that concentrations of all three gases have increased — but each tells a different story about its sources.

Why the South? — The Population Density Context

Spatial Context

Population Distribution Explains the Pattern

The CO and NO₂ distribution maps are not random — they closely mirror Ghana's population density map. Greater Accra, with 300–1,681 persons per km², is by far the most densely populated region, and it is also the primary hotspot for both gases.

High population density drives the key emission sources: greater vehicular traffic (many older, less efficient imported vehicles), expanded industrial activity, and higher energy demand. Ashanti Region, centred on Kumasi, shows the second highest density and the second highest pollutant concentrations — a direct correspondence.

The sparse northern regions (19–85 persons per km²) show correspondingly low CO and NO₂ levels, reinforcing that urbanisation and population concentration are the primary spatial determinants of these two gases.

Spatial Distribution of SO₂ Concentrations — 2019 (left) vs 2024 (right) SO₂ tells a contrasting story. Unlike CO and NO₂, its distribution is more scattered across the country, with pockets of higher concentration in the northern regions. Results — Seasonal Variation

Dry Season vs Wet Season — A Consistent Pattern Across All Three Gases

Time-series charts spanning January 2019 to June 2024 reveal a striking seasonal cycle: pollutant densities peak every dry season (November–February) and drop during the wet season (March–October). This pattern is consistent across all three gases and is driven by meteorological conditions rather than changes in emission sources.

During the dry season, stable atmospheric conditions and low humidity prevent pollutants from dispersing, causing them to accumulate at ground level. The wet season reverses this: rainfall acts as a natural scrubber, washing pollutants out of the atmosphere and reducing ground-level concentrations significantly.

Carbon Monoxide (CO) — 2019 to 2024 Clear seasonal peaks each dry season. Overall upward trend across the five-year period.
Nitrogen Dioxide (NO₂) — 2019 to 2024 Consistent dry-season spikes, with the highest peaks recorded in 2022 and 2023.
Sulphur Dioxide (SO₂) — 2019 to 2024 More volatile than CO or NO₂, with a notable spike in mid-2024 suggesting increased biomass burning activity.
Results — Health Implications

The Human Cost of Air Pollution

Exposure to CO, NO₂, and SO₂ is directly linked to a range of severe health conditions. WHO Global Health Estimates data for Ghana show a clear and worrying trend: deaths from pollution-related conditions are rising, and males, young children, and the elderly bear a disproportionate burden.

Interpretation

Key Findings

🏙️

Urban South as Pollution Hotspot

Greater Accra, Kumasi, and Tema consistently show the highest CO and NO₂ concentrations, driven by vehicle emissions from a largely ageing, poorly-maintained fleet and significant industrial activity. Population density is the primary spatial predictor.

🔥

Biomass Burning Drives SO₂ in the North

Unlike CO and NO₂, SO₂ is more concentrated in the northern regions where wood and charcoal remain the dominant cooking fuels. 54.3% of Ghanaian households still relied on these fuels in 2021, down from 73.9% in 2010 — progress, but insufficient.

📈

All Three Gases Increased 2019–2024

Every pollutant measured showed higher concentrations in 2024 compared to 2019, reflecting ongoing urbanisation, a growing vehicle fleet, and limited progress in emission regulation and enforcement.

🌧️

Seasonality as a Public Health Risk

The consistent dry-season spike in all three gases means that health risks are significantly elevated for November–February every year. Public health advisories and intervention campaigns should be timed to this cycle.

👶🧓

Children and the Elderly Most Vulnerable

Children aged 0–4 (4,280 deaths in 2019) and adults aged 70+ (19,326 deaths in 2019) bear the heaviest mortality burden. Developing respiratory systems and age-related immune decline make these groups disproportionately susceptible.

🚗

Regulatory Enforcement Is the Critical Gap

Healthcare professionals interviewed for the study unanimously identified weak enforcement of vehicular emission standards — facilitated through DVLA — as the primary barrier to improving air quality. Education alone is insufficient without accountability.
