Project Overview

26 Years of Mining-Induced Land Degradation

This study provides a systematic, multi-temporal satellite assessment of land degradation and restoration evidence in Tarkwa-Nsuaem Municipality, Western Region of Ghana — one of West Africa's most intensively mined local government areas. Using Landsat imagery processed on Google Earth Engine, the study tracks land cover change and vegetation dynamics across four time points (2000, 2010, 2020, and 2026), covering the full arc from early large-scale mining expansion through peak production to the current reclamation phase.

The analysis operates at two spatial scales: the full municipal boundary and, critically, the dissolved licensed mining concession boundary obtained from the Ghana Mining Repository. This dual-scale approach enables a direct empirical assessment of whether restoration activities within formally licensed mining areas are keeping pace with ongoing land disturbance — a question with direct implications for enforcement of Ghana's Minerals and Mining Act 2006 (Act 703).

Both Goldfields Ghana Limited (Tarkwa and Damang Mines) and AngloGold Ashanti (Iduapriem Mine) operate within the study area, alongside extensive artisanal and small-scale mining (Galamsey) activity that is visible but not formally bounded in satellite imagery.

Study Area

Tarkwa-Nsuaem Municipality — Ghana's Most Intensively Mined District

Located in the Western Region of Ghana within the Birimian greenstone belt — the principal gold-bearing geological formation in the country — Tarkwa-Nsuaem Municipality has been the centre of Ghana's gold mining industry for over a century. The municipality's 1,682 km² encompasses some of West Africa's largest open-pit gold mines, extensive artisanal mining networks, moist semi-deciduous forest reserves, and the communities that live and farm alongside all of them.

Methodology

A Rigorous Multi-Temporal Remote Sensing Pipeline

All data acquisition, processing, classification, and index computation were performed in Google Earth Engine, with cartographic production and area statistics in QGIS. The mining concession overlay from the Ghana Mining Repository is what sets this methodology apart — it enables a compliance-focused analysis, not just a landscape description.

01

Multi-Sensor Data Acquisition

Landsat Collection 2 Level-2 surface reflectance imagery was used across all four time points: Landsat 7 ETM+ for 2000 and 2010, Landsat 8 OLI for 2020, and merged Landsat 8/9 for 2026. Both Tier 1 and Tier 2 scenes were merged for Landsat 7 to maximise scene availability.

02

Cloud Masking & Radiometric Scaling

Pixels flagged as cloud (bit 3), cloud shadow (bit 4), or dilated cloud (bit 1) in the QA_PIXEL band were masked using the CFmask algorithm. Optical bands were rescaled using USGS Collection 2 factors: DN × 0.0000275 − 0.2. Band standardisation was applied across sensors for temporal consistency.

03

Median Composite Generation

A pixel-wise median composite was computed for each target year from all available cloud-masked scenes, producing clean, representative surface reflectance images robust to residual cloud artefacts. Each composite was clipped to the municipal boundary.

04

Random Forest Classification

Supervised classification was performed using ee.Classifier.smileRandomForest() with six input bands (Blue, Green, Red, NIR, SWIR-1, SWIR-2) and five classes: Builtups, Dense Forest, Open Forest, Barelands, and Water. Training samples were collected independently for each time point.

05

NDVI Analysis

NDVI = (NIR − Red) / (NIR + Red) was computed for all four epochs and reclassified into three vegetation condition zones: Dense Vegetation (>0.50), Sparse Vegetation (0.25–0.50), and Non-Vegetated (<0.25). NDVI difference images were classified using the mean ± 1 SD method.

06

Mining Concession Overlay & Reclamation Analysis

Mining concession boundaries from the Ghana Mining Repository were dissolved into a single polygon. All outputs were clipped to this boundary to isolate mining-specific dynamics. Reclamation evidence was defined as Barelands → vegetation transitions within the concession, compared against new degradation (vegetation → Barelands) to produce a degradation-to-recovery ratio.

Results — NDVI Analysis

Vegetation Health: A Steadily Deteriorating Picture

The NDVI classified maps provide independent spectral confirmation of the LULC findings. Non-Vegetated and Sparse Vegetation expand progressively across all four time points, concentrated within and radiating outward from the licensed concession zones in the north. Dense Vegetation declined from 95.98% of the municipal area in 2000 to 87.39% by 2026 — a net loss of 7,804 ha of healthy vegetation cover.

NDVI Difference Maps — Direction and Magnitude of Vegetation Change

The NDVI difference maps classify vegetation change using statistically derived thresholds (mean ± 1 SD), distinguishing significant Decline (red), No Change (yellow), and Recovery (green) across each inter-period interval. The dominance of yellow confirms broad landscape stability in non-mined areas, while the red patches concentrated within and around the concession boundary confirm the persistence and acceleration of vegetation loss in mining zones.

Results — Reclamation Evidence

Is Restoration Keeping Pace with Degradation?

The central policy question of this study is whether mining companies are reclaiming disturbed land at a rate commensurate with the scale of ongoing disturbance. Reclamation evidence was defined as any transition from Barelands to Open Forest or Dense Forest within the licensed concession boundary between consecutive time points, quantified and compared against new degradation (any vegetation class transitioning to Barelands) in the same period.

3.78 : 1

For every hectare of vegetation recovery recorded within the licensed concession boundary over 26 years, approximately 3.78 hectares of vegetated land were converted to bare ground.

Interpretation

Key Findings

📈

Accelerating Barelands Expansion

Barelands grew by 161.5% across the municipality over 26 years — from 5,208 ha (5.75%) in 2000 to 13,621 ha (15.05%) in 2026. Critically, the rate of expansion accelerated in every successive period, reaching its highest annual rate of approximately 1,044 ha/year in 2020–2026.

🌲

Continuous Forest Loss Within Concessions

Dense Forest within the licensed concession boundary declined continuously across all four time points — from 34.42% (12,843 ha) in 2000 to 23.80% (8,880 ha) in 2026. Unlike the broader municipality, Dense Forest within concessions showed zero recovery at any time point.

⚠️

Misleading Municipality-Wide Recovery Signal

Apparent Dense Forest recovery at the municipal scale (2010–2026) is entirely attributable to natural succession in undisturbed southern areas outside the concession boundary — not to any reclamation activity. The concession-specific analysis removes this artefact.

⛏️

Galamsey Extends Degradation Beyond Concessions

The progressive spread of Barelands and low-NDVI signatures beyond the formal concession boundary across all time points confirms that unlicensed artisanal mining (Galamsey) contributes substantially to landscape-scale degradation that falls outside the regulatory framework.

📉

Reclamation Wholly Inadequate

The 3.78:1 degradation-to-recovery ratio within licensed concessions — confirmed independently by NDVI temporal difference analysis (2.45:1) — constitutes direct empirical evidence that land restoration obligations under Ghana's Minerals and Mining Act 2006 (Act 703) are not being met.

🛰️

GEE Methodology Is Replicable Nationally

With Overall Accuracy exceeding 99.5% and Kappa coefficients above 0.994 at all four time points, the Google Earth Engine workflow demonstrated in this study provides a robust, low-cost, and directly replicable template for satellite-based reclamation monitoring across all Ghanaian mining districts.
